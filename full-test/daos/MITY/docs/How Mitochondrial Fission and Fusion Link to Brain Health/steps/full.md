# Fixing Broken Mitochondria: A New Path to Brain Health

## Introducing FissionBio

Brain cells burn through energy at an extraordinary rate. Every thought, every memory, every movement depends on hundreds of mitochondria packed into each neuron.

But all that activity takes a toll. Mitochondria can become damaged, start fragmenting, and gradually lose their ability to power the cell. This isn't just a byproduct of aging – it appears to play an active role in diseases like Alzheimer's, ALS, and Huntington's.

These diseases have long been treated separately, each with its own mechanisms and drug programs. But researchers have uncovered a common thread – an interaction between the proteins Drp1 and Fis1 causing mitochondria to fragment excessively.

FissionBio, backed by community funding through Cerebrum DAO, is developing drugs targeting this interaction. They're exploring a fundamental mechanism of neurodegeneration – how mitochondrial imbalance can push neurons toward failure – and designing small molecules to stop it at the source.

Read on to see how broken mitochondria became the focus – and how decentralized funding is pushing their research forward.

## Understanding the Link Between Mitochondria and Disease

Every cell in your body contains hundreds or thousands of mitochondria, but nowhere are they more critical than in your brain.

Neurons are energy-hungry cells that never stop working, requiring a constant supply of ATP (cellular fuel) to maintain electrical activity, transport materials, and repair themselves.

In healthy cells, mitochondria maintain a delicate balance between two opposing processes: fusion and fission. This dynamic system allows mitochondria to adapt while maintaining optimal function.

How Mitochondrial Fission and Fusion Link to Brain Health

In healthy brains, fission and fusion processes work in relative harmony, maintaining networks that can meet cellular energy demands while removing damaged components.

Normal mitochondrial fission is carried out by Drp1 (dynamin-related protein 1), which functions like 'molecular scissors,' wrapping around mitochondria and constricting them until they separate.

Under these healthy conditions, Drp1 binds to receptors like Mff, MiD49, and MiD51 to facilitate essential cellular processes such as mitophagy, mitochondrial biogenesis, and energy regulation.

Neurodegenerative diseases, however, can corrupt this delicate balance. During inflammatory stress, Drp1 begins interacting with a different receptor – Fis1 (fission 1) – which sits on the mitochondrial surface but is *not* involved in healthy cellular processes.

This Drp1–Fis1 interaction triggers excessive, damaging mitochondrial splitting while fusion decreases, creating a cellular landscape littered with fragmented, dysfunctional energy producers.

Research (Guo et al., 2013; Joshi et al., 2018; Joshi et al., 2018; Qi et al., 2013; Tian et al., 2017) has shown that the inhibition of Drp1/Fis1 interaction slows the progression of numerous diseases (Alzheimer’s, ALS, Huntington’s, Parkinson’s, cardiovascular disease), providing direct evidence that targeting this protein interaction could have therapeutic benefits.

# The P110 Breakthrough

What if you could block this destructive interaction without interfering with normal mitochondrial function?

That's exactly what P110 seeks to achieve – a peptide specifically designed to disrupt the Drp1-Fis1 binding while preserving essential cellular processes.

P110 represents a fundamentally new therapeutic strategy. Rather than trying to boost energy production or remove toxic proteins – common strategies in neurodegeneration research – P110 prevents the initial mitochondrial damage that triggers cellular dysfunction.

The peptide binds to the same site on Drp1 that pathologically interacts with Fis1. By occupying this binding site, P110 prevents inflammatory stress-induced fission while allowing normal mitochondrial dynamics to continue through other Drp1 receptors (Mff, MiD49, MiD51).

---

<!-- page 5 -->

This selectivity is crucial because mitochondria require normal fission processes for quality control, mitophagy, biogenesis, and energy regulation. Since P110 only blocks the pathological Drp1–Fis1 interaction, which doesn't occur during healthy cellular processes, it preserves normal mitochondrial functions.

Over 13 years of research across 11 different disease models has built an impressive body of evidence for P110's therapeutic potential, showing remarkable benefits across multiple conditions:

*   **Huntington's disease models:** Reduced brain cell death and improved motor function, with researchers noting that "novel Drp1 inhibitor diminishes aberrant mitochondrial fission and neurotoxicity" (Qi et al., 2012).
*   **ALS patient cells:** Restored mitochondrial function and cellular viability in human neurons derived from patient models (Joshi et al., 2018).
*   **Alzheimer's disease models:** Enhanced cognitive performance and reduced neurodegeneration, with studies demonstrating that "Drp1/Fis1 interaction mediates mitochondrial dysfunction, bioenergetic failure and cognitive decline in Alzheimer's disease" (Joshi et al., 2018).
*   **Cardiac conditions:** Improved function in ischemic heart disease, such as pulmonary hypertension, showing that mitochondrial protection extends beyond the brain (Tian et al., 2017).

Recent research has revealed the extensive therapeutic reach of P110 beyond ALS, demonstrating benefits in Parkinson's disease, stroke, inflammatory bowel disease, and sepsis ([Rios et al., 2023](#)).

Dr. Luis Rios, Co-Founder and Principal Investigator of Fission Bio, and his team suggest that targeting the pathological Drp1–Fis1 interaction isn't just relevant for brain diseases – it could be fundamental in treating inflammatory conditions throughout the body.

Perhaps most importantly, P110 achieves these benefits without causing immunosuppression – a major limitation of many anti-inflammatory treatments.

## FissionBio’s Innovation Strategy

Despite P110's therapeutic potential, peptides have significant limitations as therapeutic agents.

They're difficult to manufacture at scale, expensive to produce, and struggle to cross the blood-brain barrier effectively. Most importantly, peptides are typically broken down quickly in the body, requiring frequent dosing or complex delivery systems.

This is where [FissionBio](https://www.fissionbio.com) enters the picture. The company is developing small molecule drugs that mimic P110's beneficial effects while overcoming the practical limitations of peptide therapy.

Building on years of P110 research, Dr. Luis Rios made a crucial discovery by identifying the specific binding site on the Drp1 protein where P110 and small molecules can interfere with the Fis1 interaction.

He calls this the SWAG (switch I-adjacent groove) binding site – a precise molecular target that hadn't been recognized before. From this foundation, his team developed SC9, a small 2nd-generation P110 mimetic molecule that binds to the SWAG site and mimics P110's protective effects in both laboratory and animal studies.

The advantages of small molecules such as SC9 are substantial:

*   **Manufacturing:** Chemical synthesis is more scalable and cost-effective than biological production
*   **Stability:** Small molecules resist degradation and maintain activity longer in the body
*   **Brain penetration:** Properly designed small molecules can cross the blood-brain barrier more effectively

*   **Oral delivery:** Pills are more convenient than injections and improve patient compliance

Most importantly, these compounds maintain P110's unique property of preventing pathological Drp1–Fis1 interaction without suppressing normal immune function or interfering with healthy mitochondrial dynamics.

This sets them apart from broad anti-inflammatory drugs that can compromise the body's ability to fight infections.

FissionBio's strategy is to develop a "pipeline in a pill" because the same mechanism can potentially treat multiple neurodegenerative conditions. Rather than developing separate drugs for each disease, they're targeting the common mitochondrial dysfunction that underlies them.

## What Comes Next

FissionBio has demonstrated that its scientific concept is effective in the laboratory. Now comes the process of turning promising compounds into medicines that can help patients.

### From Discovery to Medicine

Moving from laboratory success to actual medicines requires following a systematic drug discovery process. While SC9 demonstrated that small molecules can target the SWAG binding site and mimic P110's effects, it lacks the brain penetration needed for neurological applications.

FissionBio is now developing third-generation compounds that build on this knowledge while achieving better drug properties, particularly brain penetration. Starting with the validated SWAG target and binding site, the team must develop entirely new molecules that can effectively treat brain diseases.

The development process involves multiple stages:

- **Hit identification** – Screening for new compounds that bind to the SWAG site and show activity  
- **Lead optimization** – Refining molecular structures for better drug properties, especially brain penetration  
- **Safety testing** – Confirming compounds are non-toxic at therapeutic doses  
- **Pharmacokinetics** – Ensuring drugs reach their targets and clear safely from the body  
- **Disease model validation** – Demonstrating efficacy in relevant animal models  

Successful compounds advance to Investigational New Drug (IND)-enabling studies – comprehensive safety testing required before human clinical trials can begin. These studies
