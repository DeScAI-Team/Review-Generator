# Fission Bio: Fixing Broken Mitochondria to Stop Brain Disease

<img>A blue-toned image of neurons with a inset diagram showing mitochondria, and a white oval button with "FIXING BROKEN MITOCHONDRIA TO STOP BRAIN DISEASE" text.</img>

How a Stanford discovery about cellular energy became a community-funded breakthrough in treating neurodegeneration at its source.

Brain cells burn through energy faster than any other cells in your body. Every thought, memory, and movement depends on hundreds of mitochondria in each neuron, constantly producing the fuel that keeps your brain running.

This creates a problem. Mitochondria can fragment, lose efficiency, and gradually fail. Research shows this isn't solely associated with normal aging – it actively drives diseases like Alzheimer's, ALS, and Huntington's (Qi et al., 2013; Guo et al., 2013).

Fission Bio raised $400,000 through Cerebrum DAO's IP-Token model to develop drugs that repair mitochondrial dysfunction instead of treating downstream symptoms. The goal is to target the fundamental mechanism by which cellular energy production breaks down.

This is the story of how broken mitochondria became the target and how decentralized science is pushing mitochondrial fission research forward.

<header>Understanding Our Mitochondrial Balance</header>

Every cell contains mitochondria, but they're particularly critical in the brain. Neurons require constant energy production to maintain electrical activity, transport materials, and perform cellular repair.

Healthy mitochondria maintain a balance between two processes: fusion (merging into networks to share resources) and fission (dividing to meet energy demands and remove damaged components).

Neurodegenerative diseases disrupt this balance. During inflammatory stress, the protein Drp1 begins interacting with Fis1 – a receptor that triggers excessive mitochondrial fragmentation, while fusion decreases (Guo et al., 2013; Joshi et al., 2018).

<img>Electron micrographs showing mitochondria in brain tissue from Alzheimer’s disease model mice. Three panels labeled d1, d2, and d3 display fragmented mitochondria undergoing splitting, while panel e shows abnormal shapes and disrupted internal structures.</img>

(d, e) Mitochondria in brain tissue from Alzheimer’s disease model mice, with one (d) undergoing splitting and others (e) showing abnormal shapes and disrupted internal structures. Zhang et al., (2016)

The result is cells filled with fragmented, dysfunctional energy producers unable to meet metabolic demands.

Mitochondrial dysfunction has been directly linked to the progression of Alzheimer's disease, where it contributes to cognitive decline and neuronal death, as well as ALS, Huntington's disease, and Parkinson's disease (Joshi et al., 2018; Guo et al., 2013).

# The P110 Discovery

Stanford researchers developed P110 – a peptide designed to block the pathological Drp1–Fis1 interaction while preserving normal mitochondrial dynamics (Qi et al., 2013).

Thirteen years of research across 11 disease models demonstrated P110’s therapeutic potential:
* Reduced neurodegeneration in Huntington's disease models (Guo et al., 2013)
* Restored mitochondrial function in ALS patient cells (Joshi et al., 2018)
* Improved cognitive performance in Alzheimer's models (Joshi et al., 2018)
* Enhanced cardiac function in ischemic disease (Tian et al., 2017)

P110 achieved these benefits without causing immunosuppression – a major limitation of anti-inflammatory treatments.

However, peptides face several therapeutic obstacles: complex manufacturing, poor brain penetration, and rapid degradation in the body. This is where Fission Bio enters the picture.

Dr. Luis Rios, Fission Bio's Co-Founder and Principal Investigator, made a crucial breakthrough by identifying the specific binding site – termed SWAG (switch I-adjacent groove) – where P110 interferes with the Fis1 interaction.

Building on years of P110 research, his team developed SC9, a small molecule that mimics P110's protective effects while overcoming peptide limitations (Rios et al., 2023).
