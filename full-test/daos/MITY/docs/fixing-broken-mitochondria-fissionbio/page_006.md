Recent research has revealed the extensive therapeutic reach of P110 beyond ALS, demonstrating benefits in Parkinson's disease, stroke, inflammatory bowel disease, and sepsis ([Rios et al., 2023](#)).

Dr. Luis Rios, Co-Founder and Principal Investigator of Fission Bio, and his team suggest that targeting the pathological Drp1–Fis1 interaction isn't just relevant for brain diseases – it could be fundamental in treating inflammatory conditions throughout the body.

Perhaps most importantly, P110 achieves these benefits without causing immunosuppression – a major limitation of many anti-inflammatory treatments.

## FissionBio’s Innovation Strategy

Despite P110's therapeutic potential, peptides have significant limitations as therapeutic agents.

They're difficult to manufacture at scale, expensive to produce, and struggle to cross the blood-brain barrier effectively. Most importantly, peptides are typically broken down quickly in the body, requiring frequent dosing or complex delivery systems.

This is where [FissionBio](https://www.fissionbio.com) enters the picture. The company is developing small molecule drugs that mimic P110's beneficial effects while overcoming the practical limitations of peptide therapy.

Building on years of P110 research, Dr. Luis Rios made a crucial discovery by identifying the specific binding site on the Drp1 protein where P110 and small molecules can interfere with the Fis1 interaction.

He calls this the SWAG (switch I-adjacent groove) binding site – a precise molecular target that hadn't been recognized before. From this foundation, his team developed SC9, a small 2nd-generation P110 mimetic molecule that binds to the SWAG site and mimics P110's protective effects in both laboratory and animal studies.

The advantages of small molecules such as SC9 are substantial:

*   **Manufacturing:** Chemical synthesis is more scalable and cost-effective than biological production
*   **Stability:** Small molecules resist degradation and maintain activity longer in the body
*   **Brain penetration:** Properly designed small molecules can cross the blood-brain barrier more effectively
