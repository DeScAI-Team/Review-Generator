This is why Drp1–Fis1 interaction is particularly damaging in neurodegenerative diseases, where chronic inflammation drives this abnormal pathway rather than the healthy fission processes the cell actually needs.

Research (Guo et al., 2013; Joshi et al., 2018; Joshi et al., 2018; Qi et al., 2013; Tian et al., 2017) has shown that the inhibition of Drp1/Fis1 interaction slows the progression of numerous diseases (Alzheimer’s, ALS, Huntington’s, Parkinson’s, cardiovascular disease), providing direct evidence that targeting this protein interaction could have therapeutic benefits.
