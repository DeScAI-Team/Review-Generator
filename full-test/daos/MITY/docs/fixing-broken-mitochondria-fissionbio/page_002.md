How Mitochondrial Fission and Fusion Link to Brain Health

In healthy brains, fission and fusion processes work in relative harmony, maintaining networks that can meet cellular energy demands while removing damaged components.

Normal mitochondrial fission is carried out by Drp1 (dynamin-related protein 1), which functions like 'molecular scissors,' wrapping around mitochondria and constricting them until they separate.

Under these healthy conditions, Drp1 binds to receptors like Mff, MiD49, and MiD51 to facilitate essential cellular processes such as mitophagy, mitochondrial biogenesis, and energy regulation.

Neurodegenerative diseases, however, can corrupt this delicate balance. During inflammatory stress, Drp1 begins interacting with a different receptor – Fis1 (fission 1) – which sits on the mitochondrial surface but is *not* involved in healthy cellular processes.

This Drp1–Fis1 interaction triggers excessive, damaging mitochondrial splitting while fusion decreases, creating a cellular landscape littered with fragmented, dysfunctional energy producers.
